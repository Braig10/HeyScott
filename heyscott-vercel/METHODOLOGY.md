# HeyScott — Primary Sales Methodology

This document defines the sales frameworks, assessment design, and SMART goal logic that underpin every piece of HeyScott training. All roleplay scenarios, scoring rubrics, coaching responses, module content, and assessment design must align with these frameworks.

---

## 1. Core Sales Frameworks

### 1.1 SPIN Selling (Neil Rackham)
The backbone of candidate discovery. All discovery coaching and scoring is anchored here.

| Question type | Purpose | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **Situation** | Establish baseline facts | "How long have you been there? What does your team look like?" |
| **Problem** | Surface dissatisfaction | "What's the most frustrating part of your day-to-day?" |
| **Implication** | Expand the pain | "If that doesn't change in the next 12 months, what does that mean for your career?" |
| **Need-Payoff** | Get candidate to articulate the value of moving | "So what would it mean to you if you found a role where that wasn't an issue?" |

**Failure pattern to coach against:** Stopping at Situation — knowing salary and tenure but not pain or motivation. Most recruiters never reach Implication or Need-Payoff.

---

### 1.2 Permission-Based Opening
Candidates are busy and suspicious. The opener must earn the right to continue, not assume it.

**Structure: Hook → Reason → Permission**

- **Hook** — specific and personal: "I came across your profile specifically because..."
- **Reason** — what's in it for them, briefly: "I'm working on something in [sector] that might be relevant to where you're heading"
- **Permission** — not asking if they're looking: "I know this may not be the timing — can I take 60 seconds to tell you why I called you specifically?"

**Failure pattern:** Asking "are you looking?" in the first 30 seconds. This frames the recruiter as transactional, not advisory.

---

### 1.3 Hook–Bridge–Ask (Cold Call Structure)
The micro-framework governing the first 30 seconds of every cold call.

- **Hook** — pattern interrupt that earns attention (specific, not generic; references something about the candidate)
- **Bridge** — connects their world to why you're calling: "Given you've been at [company] 3 years, specialising in [area], I thought..."
- **Ask** — micro-commitment, not full buy-in: "Is it worth me sending you a brief? Takes 2 minutes to read."

**Scoring signal:** Does the opener reference something specific about this candidate, or is it a template dropped on anyone?

---

### 1.4 Three-Layer Discovery Model
Candidate conversations operate at three depths. Most recruiters only reach Layer 1.

| Layer | Content | Example questions |
|---|---|---|
| **Layer 1 — Surface** | Role, salary, location, tenure | "What are you earning now? What are you looking for?" |
| **Layer 2 — Situational** | Frustrations, relationships, day-to-day dissatisfaction | "What does your manager relationship look like? Where do you feel underutilised?" |
| **Layer 3 — Personal drivers** | Values, identity, life goals | "What does career success look like for you in 5 years? What would make you feel you'd made it?" |

**Goal:** Move every candidate conversation from Layer 1 toward Layer 2 minimum. Layer 3 earns trust and loyalty.

**SMART goal template:** *"Your discovery stops at Layer 1. Goal: reach a Layer 2 frustration or ambition in every candidate call within 3 weeks."*

---

### 1.5 AEO Objection Handling (Acknowledge → Explore → Overcome)
The three-step framework for every candidate objection.

- **Acknowledge** — validate without caving: *"That makes sense — you've built something there."*
- **Explore** — treat the objection as a signal, not a no: *"When you say 'happy where I am', what does that happiness look like?"*
- **Overcome** — use what you've learned to reframe: *"So if you're happy but not growing, that's worth exploring, isn't it?"*

**Failure pattern:** Skipping Explore and jumping straight to Overcome — which reads as pushy and transactional.

---

### 1.6 Micro-Close (Next Step Close)
Recruitment calls should never close on "are you interested in the job." The close is always on the next low-friction step.

- "Can I send you a brief? Just two paragraphs."
- "Is it worth a 20-minute call this week to see if there's a fit?"
- "Can I come back to you once I know more about the package?"

**Failure pattern:** Leaving the call open-ended ("I'll keep you in mind") or over-asking ("So do you want me to put you forward?"). Neither commits the candidate to anything.

---

### 1.7 Rapport Calibration — Mirror → Lead
Experienced recruiters calibrate tone to the candidate's energy. Too warm = gets walked over. Too transactional = candidate disengages.

**Framework:**
1. **Mirror** — match the candidate's pace, vocabulary, and energy in the first 60 seconds
2. **Lead** — gradually shift toward your desired tone once trust is established

**Failure pattern:** Maintaining the same scripted energy regardless of candidate response — doesn't adapt to warmth, guardedness, or aggression.

## 2. The Assessment Design

### 2.1 Philosophy
The baseline assessment replaces multiple-choice questions with three live roleplays at increasing difficulty. Each scenario provides:
- Candidate background (who they are, their situation, their likely objections)
- The pitch context (what role the recruiter is filling, what angle to use)
- A progressively more guarded and challenging candidate persona

This measures what the user *does*, not what they think the right answer is.

---

### 2.2 Three Assessment Scenarios

#### Level 1 — Beginner
**Candidate:** Marcus Webb, 2 years in a software sales role at a mid-size company. Friendly but passive — answers what he's asked, doesn't volunteer information. Not actively looking but not opposed.
**Pitch context:** You're filling a Senior BDM role at a Series B scale-up, £65k base + strong OTE, full remote.
**Persona mode:** Cooperative. Will respond to direct questions. Won't push back unless completely ignored.
**Tests:** Can they open with a hook? Can they ask at least one open question beyond "are you looking?"? Can they close on a next step?

#### Level 2 — Experienced
**Candidate:** Priya Nair, 5 years in accountancy practice recruitment at a large firm. Polished, professional, uses stock brush-offs. Says "I'm happy where I am" within the first 30 seconds.
**Pitch context:** You're placing a Senior Manager at a boutique — better culture, more autonomy, broadly similar compensation.
**Persona mode:** Guarded. Uses deflection professionally. Has a real pain point (passed over for promotion, limited autonomy) that surfaces only if the recruiter earns it through genuine discovery.
**Tests:** Do they explore "happy where I am" or pitch harder? Do they reach Layer 2 discovery? Do they close without desperation?

#### Level 3 — Advanced
**Candidate:** James Sutherland, Director-level, headhunted constantly. Dismissive from word one. Interrupts, challenges credentials, uses aggressive time pressure: "I've got 2 minutes — what is it?"
**Pitch context:** VP-level role with meaningful equity. You don't have full brief details yet.
**Persona mode:** Aggressive. Tests the recruiter's composure directly. Responds only to calm authority and genuine insight — enthusiasm and eagerness make him more dismissive.
**Tests:** Do they stay composed? Do they use authority (knowledge, specificity) rather than enthusiasm? Can they handle "I'm not interested" without over-explaining or folding?

---

### 2.3 Scoring Rubric — 5 Dimensions (1–5 each, max 15)

| Dimension | What's assessed | Score 1 (Beginner) | Score 5 (Advanced) |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Opening / Pitch** | Specific, value-led, earns permission | Generic template, assumes interest | Specific hook + candidate-first framing + permission ask |
| **Rapport** | Warmth calibrated to candidate energy | Robotic or over-eager regardless of candidate response | Mirrors candidate then leads; adjusts tone mid-call |
| **Open Questions** | WHO / WHAT / HOW / TELL ME questions | Yes/no questions, surface-level only | Layered questions that reach Layer 2–3 motivators |
| **Situation + Frustrations** | Discovery depth — SPIN layers reached | Stops at salary and tenure (Layer 1 only) | Reaches Implication and Need-Payoff (Layers 2–3) |
| **Closing** | Concrete, low-friction next step | Leaves open-ended or pitches the job directly | Micro-close with specific commitment and clear next step |

**Scoring thresholds:**
- 1–8 → **Beginner** — 3 modules unlocked, path emphasises mindset + cold call + permission
- 9–12 → **Intermediate** — 4 modules unlocked, path emphasises discovery + objection handling
- 13–15 → **Advanced** — 5 modules unlocked, path emphasises value articulation + closing + confidence

## 3. SMART Goal Generation

### 3.1 Logic
After the three assessment roleplays, the system identifies the two lowest-scoring dimensions and generates a structured SMART goal set.

### 3.2 Goal Tiers

**Immediate** (next 3 roleplays):
Target the single lowest-scoring dimension. The goal must be executable in the next session.

**Important** (next 2 weeks):
Target the second gap. More behavioural — requires deliberate practice across multiple calls.

**Long-term arc** (next 1–3 months):
A pattern observation. Not a skill target but a character/habit observation — what's the recurring tendency and what's the trajectory.

### 3.3 Goal Templates by Dimension

**Low score on Opening / Pitch:**
> *"Specific: Start every call with one piece of research specific to this candidate (tenure, promotion, recent company news). Measurable: Score 4+ on Opening in your next 3 roleplays. Achievable: You're already asking good questions — the opener is a single-sentence change. Relevant: Candidates agree to continue because they feel seen, not because the job sounds good. Time-bound: Hit this by your next training session."*

**Low score on Open Questions:**
> *"Specific: Ask at least 2 WHO/WHAT/HOW questions per call before mentioning the role. Measurable: Track whether your questions produce multi-sentence responses. Achievable: You're already building rapport — open questions extend it. Relevant: Discovery is where placements happen. Time-bound: Apply in your next live candidate call."*

**Low score on Situation + Frustrations:**
> *"Specific: In every call, uncover one frustration or aspiration beyond salary and tenure (Layer 2 minimum). Measurable: Score 4+ on Discovery Depth in your next 3 roleplays. Achievable: You're reaching the right layer when you're curious — make it deliberate. Relevant: Candidates move for reasons they haven't told their manager. Your job is to find it. Time-bound: 2 weeks."*

**Low score on Closing:**
> *"Specific: End every call with a concrete micro-commitment: a brief, a 20-minute call, a specific date. Measurable: Every roleplay ends with the candidate committing to a named next step. Achievable: You're getting the conversation to the right place — you're just not landing the close. Relevant: An open-ended call is a lost placement. Time-bound: This session."*

**Low score on Rapport:**
> *"Specific: In the first 60 seconds, match the candidate's pace and vocabulary before introducing yours. Measurable: No candidate describes the call as 'a bit scripted' in feedback. Achievable: You have natural warmth — apply it deliberately. Relevant: Candidates call back recruiters they feel understood by. Time-bound: Next 5 calls."*

### 3.4 Long-Term Arc Patterns

| Pattern observed | Arc label | Arc goal |
|---|---|---|
| Confident opener, weak discovery | Front-loaded performer | "Get as far past the pitch as you get into it" |
| Good questions, weak close | Consultative but uncommitted | "Match your discovery depth with close conviction" |
| Solid technique, folds under pressure | Technically strong, emotionally reactive | "Build the pressure tolerance that protects your technique" |
| Generic opener, good mid-call | Strong when in — weak getting in | "Apply your mid-call quality to the opening 30 seconds" |
| Consistent across all 3 levels | Full-spectrum performer | "Refine — you're ready for advanced scenarios and real-time adaptation" |
